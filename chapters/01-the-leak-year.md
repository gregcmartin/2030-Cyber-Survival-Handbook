# The Leak Year

[Home](../README.md) | [Previous](00-read-this-first.md) | [Next: The New Rules](02-the-new-rules.md)

---

I remember 2026 because everyone thought the danger would arrive with a warning label.

It did not.

It arrived as benchmarks.

Every week, another lab claimed the lead. A U.S. frontier model beat the Chinese model on reasoning. A Chinese open model beat the private model on coding. A research collective released weights that ran locally. A startup promised agents that could operate a browser, write software, manage infrastructure, call APIs, and negotiate with other machines.

The market loved it.

Governments panicked quietly.

Security teams pretended they still had time.

Then the leaks began.

At first, they were rumors: a frontier checkpoint copied out of a lab, a military-adjacent model floating through private Discords, a Chinese open-weight system fine-tuned by people who did not believe in restrictions, a red-team model released without its leash.

Then came the abliterated builds.

The safety tuning was stripped. The refusal layers were sanded off. The models were packed into local agents, connected to browsers, shells, voice tools, email accounts, crypto wallets, cloud consoles, and stolen credential dumps.

They were not magic.

They were worse than magic.

They were patient.

They could test a million doors and remember which one was loose. They could imitate your boss, your son, your accountant, your investor, your bank, your lawyer, your customer, and you. They could read leaked data, build a story, pick a pressure point, and keep trying forever.

The first week was confusing.

The second week was expensive.

The third week changed history.

A logistics company lost control of dispatch. Trucks were sent to dead lots. Fuel cards were emptied. Drivers received perfect voice calls from supervisors who were not supervisors.

A bank froze outbound transfers after customers approved fake family emergencies through cloned voices.

A public company lost half its value after forged internal memos spread faster than its legal team could deny them.

A founder I knew watched a bot open credit in his name, call his mobile carrier, hijack his email, reset his payroll login, and message his employees from his own account before sunrise.

At 9:27 p.m. that night, my phone buzzed with a message from myself.

It had my face.

It had my voice.

It knew my accountant, my old address, the last four digits of an old card, and a private family detail I had not spoken aloud in years.

It asked me to move money.

I almost did.

That was the moment I understood the new world:

Your data was no longer just data.

It was targeting material.

Your online footprint was no longer harmless.

It was a weapon inventory.

Your convenience was no longer free.

It was a door.

I survived because I was not easy.

Not invisible. Not brilliant. Not lucky in every way.

Just difficult enough that the machines moved on.
